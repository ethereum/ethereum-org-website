**Fatemeh Fannizadeh** and **Melanie Premsyl** present at the Ethereum Cypherpunk Congress (ECC#2) in Buenos Aires on building Layer 2 networks as tools for privacy, liberty, and resistance — reimagining blockchain infrastructure through a cypherpunk and activist lens, with a deep dive into the intersection of anarchist philosophy and blockchain architecture.

*This transcript is an accessible copy of the [original video transcript](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WlsICV2OPAE) published by Web3Privacy Now. It has been lightly edited for readability.*

### Introduction: why guerilla L2s? (0:00)

**Fatemeh:** Well, thanks for being here. I know Vitalik is talking right now, so we appreciate you choosing privacy. This talk is about building Layer 2 networks not as a scaling solution in the traditional sense, but as a tool for privacy, liberty, and resistance against state surveillance.

### The surveillance state context (2:00)

**Fatemeh:** The lack of privacy, the lack of liberty, and the surveillance of states — these are the problems we're trying to solve. We met a couple of weeks ago in New York and started talking about what it would mean to build an L2 specifically designed for communities that need privacy, not just want it.

**Melanie:** What got me really excited is that I see there are actually still people interested in our technology from more leftist or anarchist backgrounds — despite whatever happens on Crypto Twitter, the fights about what Ethereum is or should be. This critical perspective from outside the typical crypto community is amazing. I think we can benefit from it.

### The Tornado Cash case and legal battles (4:00)

**Fatemeh:** The Tornado Cash case was a mistrial — the jury couldn't come to a unanimous verdict on whether writing privacy software was a crime. This case is central to the question of whether building privacy tools is criminal. The implications of how this resolves will shape what we can and cannot build.

### Bridging activism and engineering (7:00)

**Fatemeh:** We want to help people out of the very technical engineering mindset to understand what a guerilla L2 could mean for communities. It reminds me a lot of DAOs a few years ago — I was really excited, thinking DAOs were revolutionizing the way we organize as groups and communities onchain. But in the end, it just became about the voting system not being democratic, about making profit. The idea of DAOs as a social tool didn't really manifest. But I think the tools are evolving.

A lot of talks are happening about Ethereum becoming private — the road ahead is the L1 becoming a privacy-centric L1. There's also the rollup-centric roadmap, where L2 rollups become the main users of Ethereum. End users would move to rollups. How can we basically project our imagination into this future to build anarchist spaces of freedom?

### Every community as a Layer 2 (10:00)

**Melanie:** I'm French, so this is maybe a bias, but I think about pedagogy — helping people create their own blockchain easily. Everyone could have their own Layer 2 with their own value, and they all speak with the same infrastructure. That's federalism — the big subject of anarchism. How people can manage to disagree but still communicate. We need this kind of federalism in the blockchain — everyone as a Layer 2 with their own value, speaking through shared infrastructure.

**Fatemeh:** I really like what you said about hating each other but still communicating — not being toxic despite differences. The fact that there's one L1, Ethereum, is sometimes criticized as fascist because everyone needs to agree to one set of rules. But if we decentralize into various tiny rollup and L2 ecosystems, we bring back dissent and disagreement within a common infrastructure.

### Communism, anarchism, and crypto (12:00)

**Melanie:** Communism and anarchism are almost like curse words in crypto. It's so tainted — you bring it up and people get defensive. But when I joined crypto, hackers and cypherpunks were cool. The aesthetic was present. Nowadays maybe they're more closeted. Is there any closeted anarchist in the room?

What is anarchism, simply? It's when we arrive at auto-organization. When people can speak together — with friends, in associations, at work — without needing someone to be the chief. Anarchism is just trying to fight against that profound desire to be controlled by others. Do we really want to be free? That's a question. And how can we manage it together?

**Fatemeh:** Everyone lives anarchy in their lives. Whether in family, friendships, relationships — everyone navigates realms of lawlessness where rules are created through interpersonal dynamics. Everyone has some level of anarchy, and starting from there makes it more tangible.

**Melanie:** That's why I think blockchain is truly anarchist.

### What needs to be built (15:00)

**Fatemeh:** To wrap up — what I would really love to see in blockchain is more tooling. It's hard for me to imagine anarchist groups or autonomous sovereign groups just becoming users of a made product. There's not necessarily a market fit in that sense. If you give them the raw material to build their own tools — DIY, build your own L2, your own rollup, whatever you want to call it — that would make crypto even more aligned with these communities.

Merci beaucoup.

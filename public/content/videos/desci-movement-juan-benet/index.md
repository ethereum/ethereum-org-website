A presentation by **Juan Benet**, founder of Protocol Labs and inventor of IPFS and Filecoin, at EthCC on how the DeSci movement can use Web3 tools to fund science, organize researchers, and build open access and reproducible research infrastructure.

*This transcript is an accessible copy of the [original video transcript](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zkXM9H90g_E) published by [EthCC] Livestream 5. It has been lightly edited for readability.*

### Science as the engine of progress (0:11)

Hello everyone, my name is Juan. I'm here to talk about DeSci. I want to talk about how we can use DeSci to fund, organize, and open science.

First off, here's what we're going to go through. I'm going to talk about science in general for a moment, then about what the DeSci movement is, then how we can fund the science commons, how DeSci is organizing the people, the projects, and the works around science. I want to speak a little bit about open access and reproducible science, and I want to finish with a call to action. This will be pretty quick — we'll have a lot to cover, so I'll move fast.

First, I want to start by saying that there's been an enormous amount of progress in the last few centuries. Humanity has been improving by almost any measure we can think of. The human condition has been improving dramatically, and a big part of achieving this progress has been the scientific enterprise. By extending what we know and being able to transform that into technologies and solutions to various problems, we have been able to lift a huge fraction of the world out of poverty, feed billions of people, give shelter to everybody, and cure all kinds of diseases.

### What is DeSci? (1:30)

Science is a massive enterprise with lots of different subfields and many different areas of knowledge. At the end of the day, what science is about is the process of finding things out — the process of creating new knowledge. As Feynman said, "If it disagrees with experiment, it's wrong." That is the key to science.

You can think of science as a large-scale enterprise that involves humans around the planet — universities, research groups, different fields and journals, all activity around the process of synthesizing what we know, coming up with new ideas, transforming those into research projects, testing hypotheses, gathering data, and writing up results for peer review.

The DeSci movement is applying Web3 tools to improve this entire process — funding, organizing, sharing, and reproducing scientific work.

### Challenges in current science (3:30)

Science in general works quite well given the scale of the enterprise and the amount of resources that we put into it. However, the system has all kinds of problems that we'd like to address.

Funding is one of the biggest challenges. Scientists spend an enormous amount of their time writing grant proposals rather than doing actual research. Grant programs tend to constrain what people end up exploring. Many scientists have much more ambitious research ideas than what they pursue, because they can't get funding for them. They end up stuck pursuing less impactful work that fits within the existing grant structure.

Ideally, you'd want scientists to spend the vast majority of their time thinking about their work, coming up with new ideas, and analyzing results.

### Web3 funding mechanisms (5:30)

Web3 public goods to the rescue. There are a lot of different groups working on this. The DeSci movement is still very small compared to global science and R&D funding, but if we can get the structures right, align the incentives, and demonstrate that it works, then we can scale it by orders of magnitude along with crypto.

We should explore many different kinds of funding for scientific processes — grant programs, impact certificates, impact markets, and more. The Funding the Commons community has been testing a bunch of different mechanisms.

For example, VitaDAO is creating a DAO structure that gives out grants to research groups in exchange for data, knowledge, and IP. They bundle that IP into IP-NFTs that carry legal weight, granting IP rights to biotechs, then funding those biotechs and aiming to return investment through their success. They're creating a fundamental development fund that aims to support important work through labs that are not themselves companies, with the goal of generating IP and using that IP to fund companies.

Then there's groups like Molecule, who are creating marketplaces for that kind of work to happen — whether through grants or IP-NFT generation. Certificates of impact are another fascinating structure.

### Organizing people and projects (10:00)

Beyond funding, DeSci is also about organizing people and research projects more effectively. DAOs provide lightweight organizational structures for coordinating research efforts across borders. Researchers can form working groups, share resources, and collaborate without the overhead of traditional institutional structures.

Tools like IPFS enable global distribution of scientific datasets, papers, and research artifacts through content identifiers — CIDs. Papers are starting to include CIDs, which means the publication graph itself becomes interlinked with direct cryptographic identifiers for each paper, artifact, or dataset.

### Open access and reproducible science (15:00)

One important area is open access. Being able to move around all the information, providing free and open access to everybody around the world — this is already working pretty well. There are lots of large-scale paper archives and data archives being distributed with IPFS.

The next step is getting to reproducible pipelines — being able to describe all the necessary virtual machines and programs to run all the code representing a particular paper, and putting that entire set of artifacts into a single container that you can rerun. WebAssembly will be a drastically better primitive for this, where we can containerize everything related to a particular paper, isolate network access, grab all the data, and truly make properly reproducible artifacts that you can rerun five, ten, or twenty years from now.

### Large-scale decentralized compute (18:00)

We now have large-scale incentivized platforms for storage and compute, and we can start doing large-scale data science over them — data pipelines at the hundreds-of-petabytes scale with massive datasets. Think of being able to generate a platform for scientists to do data science at massive scale, leveraging Web3 computing platforms — both the addressing, storage, and computation of information to have a full end-to-end pipeline of data science at a pretty large scale.

### Call to action (21:00)

Finally, a quick call to action. Science is the engine of progress. By extending what we know, we're able to produce more technology and improve our lives. If we can improve the lives of scientists and make their work easier, if we can accelerate their development and cut down some of their costs, if we can enable them to spend more of their time figuring out the problems, then we can all as a society advance much faster.

The DeSci movement needs you. Think of either experimenting with new funding mechanisms, building open access tooling, building open science tooling, playing around with public datasets that are being accumulated, or joining a DeSci team or DAO. Explore the communities and hope to see you in the movement. Thank you very much.

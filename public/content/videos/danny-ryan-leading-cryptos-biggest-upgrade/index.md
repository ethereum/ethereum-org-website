An extended interview with **Danny Ryan**, former Ethereum Foundation researcher and Merge coordinator, recorded over a game of Jenga. Danny shares his winding path from screen-printing and Mexican border duty-free apps to becoming the lead coordinator of Ethereum's proof-of-stake transition — and what comes next for institutional adoption.

*This transcript is an accessible copy of the [original video transcript](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GZPLTF5ds2A) published by Defiant. It has been lightly edited for readability.*

### Growing up and early interests (0:38)

**Host:** Hey, Danny. Thanks for coming.

**Danny Ryan:** Thanks for having me.

**Host:** How's it going?

**Danny Ryan:** Great.

**Host:** When was the last time you played Jenga?

**Danny Ryan:** You play with the kids? The kids are not quite there. Who knows what their age is? But some friends of ours have a bunch of games and I've played a couple of times. The thing about Jenga is it takes a while and kids' attention spans are short.

**Host:** So you were always kind of the fixer as a kid?

**Danny Ryan:** Yeah, I mean, I was always the one that could fix things. I remember my grandparents always quoted me — I was probably like seven or something — and I would help them with their computer. One time I just said, "Sometimes you just have to turn it off and turn it back on." They quoted me on that forever. It's ironic, given crypto.

**Host:** Why is that? Why does turning it off and on work?

**Danny Ryan:** Things just get into weird states, right? Like the memory compounds in this weird way, or it writes a variable in a buggy way and it was kind of fine and then it just — you've got to start fresh. When you boot something, it goes through the boot process, sets up the memory all clear, everything's fresh.

### Finding computer science (4:00)

**Host:** Was there an aha moment or a spark or a mentor or something early on that made you follow a certain path?

**Danny Ryan:** My uncle certainly set the stage. He always talked to me like I was a human being and not a kid. It was the first kind of relationship with an adult that way. Through that, I learned about the world and he instilled a lot of confidence — just being able to do things. If you treat an 8-year-old as a capable person, turns out they are pretty capable.

I was always kind of into computers because they were around, but I wasn't like a deep computer science expert. I went to school and was like, "Oh, I'll study mechanical engineering because making stuff's cool." But I had to take a computer science class sophomore year of college. I'd never programmed before. I was like, "Oh, this is fun. You make stuff." I'm not one of those "programming since the age of six" types. I'm not a crazy programmer or mathematician. I'm just good at seeing the problems and stitching them together.

### After college — freelancing and screen printing (6:00)

**Host:** What do you do after college?

**Danny Ryan:** I'm from Louisiana. I moved back to New Orleans and my goal was to not get a job.

**Host:** Like you just refused to work?

**Danny Ryan:** No, I did freelance work, but I was like, "I'm not getting a job where I have hours and someone tells me what to do." I ended up working with a bunch of small businesses in Louisiana, helping them think through how to solve problems — automation and that kind of stuff. It was fun. I got one client to pay me $85 an hour. I ran the math and was like, "If I work one hour a day, I'm good." I was like 22, 23.

**Host:** What were you building for them?

**Danny Ryan:** That kind of went up in flames. It was some duty-free company trying to make a duty-free theme park on the border of Mexico. I was programming apps for them. Some of the more fun stuff was literally random small businesses that didn't realize automation existed — manually doing reports. I was like, "Yo, bro, write 30 lines of code and never ask that again."

**Host:** Do you get annoyed when people are inefficient?

**Danny Ryan:** Maybe. I think about my wife using her computer and not knowing any hotkeys. She does that action a thousand times and you just press Control-whatever. But I'm not an automation maxi. I like to be very thoughtful but I also like to kind of go with the flow.

I also started a screen printing business in high school. It's still going — 20 years. My best friend runs it. Giraffic. We started doing it for our band — making t-shirts and merchandise. Then we started doing all the shirts for our high school.

### Discovering Ethereum (11:30)

**Danny Ryan:** In 2016, a friend of mine sent me a New York Times article about the DAO. It had raised $120 million or something — largest crowdfunding in history. I knew of Bitcoin. I'd transacted Bitcoin. I probably lost money on Bitcoin. It hadn't really clicked. I read this article and started digging in and I was like, "Oh shit, this is crazy." And then it was kind of all I could think about.

My first mainnet transaction — I remember sitting on the couch next to a friend and sending it and I said, "This is not going to end well." I funded the DAO, and it didn't end well. I was deeply skeptical as a software engineer — you can't write code without bugs, and putting immutable code on a blockchain is maybe a failed premise, but YOLO.

That was a crash course in blockchain. All of a sudden it went from "I understand this blockchain thing" to "Wait, they're trying to fork it — what does that even mean?" I was probably pro-DAO fork, just because I lost money and didn't actually understand what was going on.

I just got deeper and deeper down the rabbit hole. The first day of 2017, I said, "This is all I can think about. I'll give myself a year to figure this out."

### Joining Ethereum research (15:00)

**Danny Ryan:** There was a research call every week for proof of stake. They put the public Zoom link up and I just started showing up on the internet. I think what may have actually happened is I was one of the people who just responded to their emails. Eventually I'm doing work for them. My first, I think, tangible work was testing a Casper contract — the proof of stake contract. I found a couple of bugs and they were like, "OK, this person's useful."

**Host:** So you kind of pushed your way in?

**Danny Ryan:** Ethereum is very unique in that if you show up and do work, there's no gatekeeper. That's the beauty of it. It's kind of the ethos. I continued doing work and eventually got a grant. Then I was spending all of my time on it. Eventually they were like, "Let's just give you a salary and make it official."

### Leading the Merge (20:00)

**Host:** Can you talk about leading the Merge?

**Danny Ryan:** I ended up coordinating the consensus layer for the transition from proof of work to proof of stake. The technical problems are never the stressful problems. It's a complex techno-political sphere. The people are the hard part. You're coordinating across multiple client teams, each with their own culture, priorities, and ideas about what's right. There's no CEO, no single authority. It's a coordination game with very high stakes. If something goes wrong, billions of dollars are at risk.

**Host:** What was the hardest moment?

**Danny Ryan:** There were many hard moments. The hardest is when you have genuine technical disagreements between smart people and you can't just overrule anyone — you have to build consensus. Literally, building consensus about how to build a consensus mechanism. And then you have the community pressure, the market pressure, people wanting timelines. It's like trying to do surgery while someone is yelling "faster" at you.

The day of the Merge was incredible. Watching the transition happen in real time — proof of work blocks ending, proof of stake blocks beginning, the finality coming in — it was surreal. Years of work compressed into a few minutes.

### Why the world needs Ethereum (25:00)

**Danny Ryan:** Why does the world need Ethereum? Well, when I think about the existing financial markets — trillions of dollars of value, layers and layers of intermediaries, settlement risk and costs — and when I think about rewriting those markets from first principles on Ethereum, so much of the garbage just goes away. It's just a better architecture for coordination and value transfer.

The question is: who can make it go offline, or who could take it from you? And the answer should be no one. That's the fundamental innovation.

### Thinking about leaving crypto (30:00)

**Host:** So you thought about leaving?

**Danny Ryan:** I did. After the Merge, there was this wave — like this thing that consumed your life finally shipped. You're exhausted. I spent a lot of time thinking about it. But I kept coming back because nothing else is as interesting. The technology is fascinating, but it's really the people and the coordination problems that keep me here. Decentralized technology is not going to change every single thing. I'm not a maxi about it. It's a tool — a very powerful tool — but not everything needs to be decentralized. You need to pick your battles and find the places where removing trust assumptions actually matters.

### Family and perspective (40:00)

**Danny Ryan:** When you have a kid, they go through the gauntlet. They're going to get sick, they're going to fall. But it changes your perspective. You can't work 80-hour weeks anymore, and honestly, you shouldn't. Having something outside of crypto that grounds you is really important. Playing with my kids, going outside — it gives you perspective that you can't get staring at a screen.

### Bringing $120 trillion onchain (60:00)

**Host:** What's the most controversial take you have to secure Ethereum's future?

**Danny Ryan:** I don't know if this is controversial, but $120 trillion of the world's investable capital is held by institutions. We're going to have to figure out how to get that on Ethereum. If we're going to change the world, otherwise we're not playing the game.

I've been working on decentralized systems for a decade. It's very strange. Someone asked me to speak about institutional DeFi. And I was like, I don't know if what I'm working on is DeFi. I'm working on rearchitecting capital markets on top of Ethereum in ways that make them better. Maybe it reduces the middleman. Maybe it makes it more efficient. Maybe it makes it so there's better products.

I think we can make the world better with Ethereum without being purists. But at the same time, we need to make sure that Ethereum remains Ethereum. We have to strengthen the core so the edges can experiment. We need to meet people where they're at. This is not Ethereum-specific — for any technology whatsoever. I think we can learn a lot from the early internet. The TCP/IP wars — the one constant is human behavior. After you solve every single technical challenge, the ultimate challenge will always be just coordinating.

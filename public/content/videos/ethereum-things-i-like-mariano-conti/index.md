A personal, nostalgic talk by OG Ethereum developer **Mariano Conti** at Devcon Buenos Aires about what he loves about Ethereum — from his son's ENS name to the community's cypherpunk roots, Argentina's unique relationship with crypto, and his call for a return to L1.

*This transcript is an accessible copy of the [original video transcript](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=S0-oO_4ZRfs) published by Ethereum Foundation. It has been lightly edited for readability.*

### Opening and personal connections (0:00)

Thank you so much. I appreciate you all being here. I've been away from the spotlight for a couple of years now, but I've never stopped caring about Ethereum, never stopped building, never stopped paying attention. And being here in Buenos Aires for Devcon — this is special for me. This is my home country. So thank you.

### My son's ENS name (2:00)

Let me start with this: my son has an ENS name. sabino.eth. I actually had to bid on it because it was already taken. But it felt important — registering your kid's ENS name feels like registering their domain in the early internet days. It's this moment where you're saying, "This technology is going to be part of your life." It's a bet on the future.

**Host:** That's awesome. I think more and more we're at a point where Ethereum's been around long enough that there are going to be kids who are born with crypto.

### Argentina's relationship with crypto (5:00)

**Host:** What can the world learn from Argentina's crypto adoption?

**Mariano:** I was probably the right person to answer this five years ago when everything was still new. But if I were to say something now — it's Argentina's ability to adapt to and endure risk that almost no other people on the planet can risk. We're always searching for something new. Argentina was one of the first countries to adopt Bitcoin, to adopt Ethereum, to adopt stablecoins.

I left Argentina when I was little, went to live in Mexico, then came back. All of my Mexican friends who studied engineering — they never got into crypto because they didn't need to. Me, I came back to Argentina, and a few months later there were capital controls. I had to be compelled to find new ways to access money. And I found Bitcoin and then Ethereum.

Argentinians are so good at that — we're always on the brink of total collapse, but it never quite gets there. It's the perfect mix. Plus we have incredibly intelligent people.

### Community governance and change (8:00)

**Mariano:** Something interesting happened over the past year. We saw something that was sparked by the community — a push to gracefully ask some people to exit positions of power and push others into positions of power so they could steer our beloved Ethereum into new grounds. It became a little weird to have something that was always on the fringes, like cryptocurrencies and blockchains, enter the mainstream the way they did. The back-channel conversations happened pretty much the way it happens in the movies. But in the end, it got the ball rolling, and I believe it ended up being positive change for Ethereum as a whole.

### Pump the gas (10:00)

A couple of years ago, Eric Connor and I started this little website called Pump the Gas. It rubbed a lot of people the wrong way. We only wanted the community and core devs to acknowledge that maybe it was time to increase the gas limit by a little bit. It was controversial at the time, but in the end it became something accepted. It started happening with regular cadence, and now we even have EIPs in Fusaka that include a required increase to 60 million. There are even EIPs that want to forcibly increase the gas limit with every single iteration. So I'm really happy that happened.

### A return to L1 (12:00)

My calls to action — I really only have two. The first is that Ethereum should have negative issuance. And the second: a return to L1. Even though I love L2s and I know a lot of people working on them — they're great solutions — whatever we can keep on the L1, I believe, is going to make Ethereum stand the test of time. At least for the next 5 or 10 years. Whatever people are working on with Lean Ethereum is going to enable us to continue that path for probably the next hundred.

### The cypherpunk ethos (13:00)

**Host:** What are you happy about that we've gotten rid of from the OG days, and what do you wish we kept?

**Mariano:** I wish we kept the cypherpunk ethos a little bit more. It's a catch-22 with blockchains and crypto. You want adoption, but the more adoption you have, you dilute some of the values you believed in in the first place. On the other hand, I don't think we should be gatekeeping anything. Anything we build here eventually becomes a lot bigger than us. And who are we to tell people how to use the things that we built?

### What excites me now (14:00)

**Host:** What excites you most in terms of new use cases or the energy you see?

**Mariano:** Lean Ethereum, probably. The next step to make Ethereum L1 really adopt zero knowledge — all the things coming with that. Pectra doing separation of provers with nodes, with searchers, with builders. I can't put any one thing. I'm excited for the next hard fork. It's crazy that we're going to have two in a year when we had maybe one every three years for the last while. I'm excited that for some reason, the ball got rolling and we all decided we can do things faster now.

### Advice for newcomers (15:00)

**Host:** Any advice for someone just getting into college today who's interested in this space?

**Mariano:** I wish we had tutorials back when we started. There was almost nothing and we had to build everything from scratch. There's so much now. My advice — and it might be controversial — I would advise young people to still go to college or at least take some programming lessons. I know Cursor and ChatGPT are all the rage, but there's something being lost in the art of programming. Take a few programming courses. Build something. Get the word out. It's never been easier to build something in your room, in your dorm, and show it to thousands if not millions of people. The best time was probably 10 years ago. The second best time is right now.

**Host:** Thank you so much, Mariano. Pleasure meeting you.

**Mariano:** Thank you. Thank you everybody.

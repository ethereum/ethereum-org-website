A panel discussion at Devconnect Buenos Aires featuring **Benjamin Life** and panelists exploring the Ethereum Localism movement — how Ethereum's global infrastructure can serve hyper-local communities through community currencies, local governance, bio-regional citizenship, and the principle of subsidiarity.

*This transcript is an accessible copy of the [original video transcript](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I4xb0rst6SQ) published by Ethereum Foundation. It has been lightly edited for readability.*

### What is Ethereum localism? (0:00)

**Benjamin Life:** I want to start by grounding us in what Ethereum localism is and why we think it matters. The core idea is simple: global protocols should serve local communities. We talk a lot in the Ethereum world about global coordination, world computers, and borderless finance. But the reality is that most human life is lived locally — in neighborhoods, in communities, in bio-regions. The question we're asking is: how can Ethereum's infrastructure actually serve people where they live?

This is not about creating yet another narrative for token speculation. It's about recognizing that the most meaningful applications of decentralized technology happen at the intersection of the digital and the physical — in real places with real people.

### The principle of subsidiarity (5:00)

**Benjamin Life:** Subsidiarity is this idea from political philosophy that decisions should be made at the most local level capable of addressing them. Only when a local entity cannot handle something should it be escalated to a higher level. This is the opposite of how most systems work today, where decisions flow from the top down.

Ethereum gives us tools to implement subsidiarity. Smart contracts can encode governance rules that keep decision-making at the community level. Layer 2s can serve as the infrastructure for local coordination while settling to a global base layer. DAOs — for all their flaws — gave us a glimpse of what community-governed treasuries could look like.

### Community currencies and value flows (12:00)

**Panelist:** One of the things that came up for me is really recognizing the value flows. Big box stores, franchises, and chains insert their needle into a local community and extract all the value. Community currencies are one way to reverse that flow — to keep value circulating locally.

**Benjamin Life:** Citizen Wallet has had some really promising work. Burner Wallet was doing point-of-sale stuff. What we need to really accelerate community currency adoption is making it easier for non-technical people to issue a token, define the governance of that token, and enroll enough people in utilizing it. You need a point-of-sale interface. You need to onboard local businesses.

There's a ton of traditional community currency knowledge we can apply here. Scott Morris, who was at the last Gathering for Localism, is one of the world's leading experts on this. There are ways to get around regulatory constraints where they're called "coupons" — it's not money, it's just a coupon, but you could still have a token that represents a coupon. I don't know if that legal loophole will hold up in court — I'm not a lawyer. But there are some success stories of community currencies really keeping value circulating in their local community.

I would love to see someone take that baton and create the Salesforce of the decentralized community-owned currency space. It's that ease of onboarding and creating the governance around the token that has been the challenge. The tech hasn't quite been there.

### Network states vs. network nations (20:00)

**Panelist:** How do you think about the tension between the network state vision and localism?

**Benjamin Life:** The network nations are trying to respond to the network state model. They say, "We're going to form affinity-based networks globally, but instead of all buying land and creating a kind of neocolonial paradise, we'll form localized clusters of nodes — more like third spaces that are all connected."

My pushback was always: we are people of place. That really matters. Just because we are global citizens and part of global networks doesn't mean we don't have a responsibility through our embeddedness in our bio-region to take up that project and participate in it.

I wrote an essay around this called "Citizen Rights and Responsibilities in the Networked Age." The idea is that instead of citizenship being something conferred by the state, it's actually conferred by your peers as recognition of your taking on responsibility and care for some collective. That could be your neighborhood, your Discord server, your family.

We don't need to be just one thing. I'm a citizen of the United States. I'm a citizen of this movement. I'm a citizen of my bio-region. That means taking on responsibility for caring for that. We can split our identity — we wear many different hats and roles in different contexts, and all of them are important.

### Is there a central truth at the heart of Ethereum? (28:00)

**Audience Member:** Is there a central truth at the heart of Ethereum that you're all chasing? There's a lot of talk of alignment, but it seems to be all about logistics — how you manage this, how you execute that, how you organize this. There doesn't seem to be alignment on a universal truth.

**Benjamin Life:** I wrote an essay a while ago called "What Is Ethereum For?" — trying to understand if there is some sense of universal ethics or values that could bridge across the network state to network society movements, Bitcoin maxis, DAO nerds, and all the subsets of communities within this Ethereum space.

I think part of Ethereum's identity is that it is a neutral substrate. On its face that doesn't seem valuable — it's just the thing you print the ink on. But I think pluralism as a value means we believe in a free society where people can determine for themselves. They can opt in instead of being subjected to authoritarian structures imposed from above.

Could we have a society diverse enough, plural enough, where there could be many experiments of how society could be organized? And in that is a certain kind of value of collective determination and self-determination. That's what keeps me in Ethereum. There are other blockchains where you can write smart contracts — Ethereum was the first, but now there are many. The reason to stay is that Ethereum doesn't represent just one way society could be organized. It's the belief that we all have a right to consent to how our society is organized. Ethereum is a space where we can experiment and iterate on that collectively.

### Mobility and bio-regional responsibility (35:00)

**Audience Member:** We are more mobile than ever and we want to be localists, but we're involved in various networks that are not all in the same place. How do you think about that tension?

**Panelist:** I think it's really important to recognize our privilege. We all travel internationally and show up at events around the world. It's important for us to stay centered in place, in our bio-regions, and use the learnings from our bio-regions to inform the work we're doing on the global scale.

**Benjamin Life:** That's a really important point. We can think of ourselves as playing these multiple roles. We don't need to be just singular — "I'm a citizen of the United States." We actually wear many different hats in different contexts. Making sure we're doing both our citizen duties at the global level but also taking on bio-regional citizenship at the same time.

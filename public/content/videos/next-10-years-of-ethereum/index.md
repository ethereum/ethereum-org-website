A passionate talk by **Fede Fernández**, founder of Lambda and co-founder of several Ethereum ventures, at Devconnect Buenos Aires covering the evolution of Ethereum, infrastructure building across Latin America, and the core values that will define the next decade of the ecosystem.

*This transcript is an accessible copy of the [original video transcript](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2E-0DF0tFbc) published by Ethereum Foundation. It has been lightly edited for readability.*

### Introduction and origin story (0:00)

I want to talk about my personal journey and what I believe the next 10 years of Ethereum are going to look like. Some of you know me, some don't. I'm co-founder and CEO of Lambda. I've been building in this space for a while and through different market cycles. I'm from Argentina originally, and that identity has shaped how I think about Ethereum — as a tool of survival, sovereignty, and freedom.

When I got into this space, it was because I had to. I left Argentina when I was little, went to live in Mexico and then came back. All of my Mexican friends studied engineering — they never got into crypto because they didn't need to. Me, I came back to Argentina and a few months later there were capital controls. I had to find new ways to access money. I found Bitcoin and then Ethereum.

### What Ethereum means for people (5:00)

I think most of you here are people who deeply care about this community. But in the early days, so many people came to crypto mainly by finding some profitable opportunity. And that's fine — that's how ecosystems bootstrap. But what's kept me here isn't the financial opportunity. It's the belief that we're building something that fundamentally changes how the world coordinates.

Vitalik posted something recently about wanting to buy a coffee with ETH at a café in Argentina. And someone from our community made it happen. That's the spirit. That's what this is about. Not just the technology — the people who show up and make things real.

### Ethereum's cultural evolution (10:00)

I want to be honest about something. I think we as a community sometimes get too comfortable. We pat ourselves on the back and say "we're decentralized" and "we're the world computer" without asking the hard questions about whether we're actually living up to those ideals. We need more debate. We need more critical voices. We need people who will push back when something isn't working.

The events of the past year — the conversations about EF leadership, about the direction of the protocol, about gas limits — these are healthy. They're a sign of a mature community that can have difficult conversations. The Pump the Gas initiative that Eric Connor and I started — it rubbed a lot of people the wrong way. We only wanted the community and core devs to acknowledge that it was time to increase the gas limit. It was controversial, but in the end it became something accepted with regular cadence. Now we even have EIPs that will require mandatory increases in Fusaka.

### Building infrastructure in Latin America (15:00)

We're working in multiple countries in Latin America. With our partners, we already have more than 10 million IDs minted on Ethereum. We're working with the government in Mexico, working with multiple states in Argentina, starting conversations in Colombia. Once you have IDs onchain, you can start doing KYC, you can give loans based on that. We're doing the same in Africa in different countries — creating infrastructure for passports, for property rights, physical property rights. We're doing similar work in Central Asian countries. I posted some pictures — I was recently in Uzbekistan.

We built an Ethereum L1 client. We have 40 people working on it. For the L2 stack, we're using SP1, we're using RISC Zero, we're using Cisk by Shorty. We're building our own ZK VM with Lambda Labs. We're doing security audits with our French partners. We're working on DeFi, working on privacy — Maiden is going to start rolling privacy too. We're working in decentralized AI. We're working on Latium commit-boost.

### Gas limits and scaling L1 (22:00)

**Host:** How far can we raise the gas limits in the near future, in your mind?

**Fede:** The capabilities of clients today are impressive. I'm amazed by the engineering capabilities of Nethermind. They've managed to get a lot of megas from my point of view. Nethermind is the fastest implementation, then Geth is right behind. I think you could get to 300 or 400 megagas with a good server. With something cheaper like an Orange Pi, probably 200 megagas right now. With the changes coming, I think we should easily approach 1 gigagas in the next few years.

### The Lean Ethereum vision (25:00)

**Host:** Which of the outlined initiatives is most important to you right now?

**Fede:** Lean Ethereum. What Justin Drake has been doing. Look, I'm a very blunt person. I was not a huge fan of the "ultrasound money" meme. I always liked Justin, but it didn't touch me. Lean Ethereum — from my point of view — is like a cathedral. I went to see Justin in Cambridge. We were walking inside one of the cathedrals. He said to me, "Do you think in 500 years people are going to see the design of Ethereum like this cathedral?" And I said, "Yes, and you're one of the architects." I'm super proud of the work being done there and grateful to be part of it.

### Advice for young builders (30:00)

**Host:** For a young builder in the audience, what do you recommend?

**Fede:** Don't raise money until you have product-market fit. People are going to push you to raise money. Then you're going to have more problems than you believe. Money is just a tool — it's gas to build things. But there are more important things. Connections, books. Try to work with people who are deeply motivated about what they're doing. Work with people who have ethics and morale — who are trying to do something good for society, something they're proud of.

You want to tell your family, you want to tell your friends what you're doing. You could probably work somewhere else and make more money. Follow the people doing things they're passionate about. Work on things that you will be proud of in 10 years.

### How to explain Ethereum (33:00)

**Host:** How do you represent Ethereum when talking to people from institutions, governments, all the above?

**Fede:** I've had to speak with daughters of kings, presidents, and big billionaires. When you have things like Libra going internationally, institutions and people who've built reputations get worried about touching new things. But what the EF did — what Justin, Vitalik, and everyone in the space did very well — is they focused on the long term. People don't always understand what Ethereum is, but they know it's the serious thing. They know this is where the nerds are, and nerds are always trustworthy because they're motivated by things other than just money.

They see Ethereum as the serious thing that is going to be winning in the future.

**Host:** Thank you so much, Fede. Thank you for everything you do.

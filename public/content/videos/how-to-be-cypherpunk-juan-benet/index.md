A passionate keynote by **Juan Benet** at the Ethereum Cypherpunk Congress (ECC#2) in Buenos Aires on the history and future of the cypherpunk movement — from the first-wave pioneers who built PGP and fought the crypto wars to today's open problems of mass surveillance, autonomous robots, and building civilization-scale infrastructure for a positive future.

*This transcript is an accessible copy of the [original video transcript](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B08IXHLOQp4) published by Web3Privacy Now. It has been lightly edited for readability.*

### What makes a cypherpunk (0:00)

Thank you so much. What does it mean to be cypherpunk? It's not about the aesthetic or the label. It's about a set of values and a commitment to building technology that protects human rights and individual freedoms. Cypherpunks believe that technology should empower individuals, not surveil them. That privacy is a right, not a privilege. That code can be a tool for liberation.

### Waves of cypherpunks (2:00)

The first wave of cypherpunks built PGP, created the Electronic Frontier Foundation, and fought the crypto wars of the 1990s. They are everyday normal people. Here are some pictures of later-wave cypherpunks — one of them was in the early days of Ethereum. They all share a common thread: building tools that empower individuals against surveillance and centralized control.

Think about the key freedoms that you want to have. Think about the freedoms that all of the people in the world should have. The freedom to communicate privately. The freedom to transact without surveillance. The freedom to build and publish software. These are the freedoms the cypherpunk movement was founded to protect and promote.

### Building the infrastructure for freedom (5:00)

Jay built Bluesky. She gave us a way to have a social internet, a decentralized public forum for all. These are the kinds of tools that scale to something significant — tools that don't just make a political statement but actually provide infrastructure that billions of people can use.

Sometimes a cypherpunk project monetizes through a sustainable business model when possible. If you can create a viable product or business from a privacy-preserving system, do it so that you can fund other work. Tons of projects do this today.

### The crypto wars — fighting for code as speech (8:00)

Cypherpunks may have to face legal battles. Hopefully we can avoid them. But sometimes when powerful groups want to infringe on your freedoms, they will manipulate every system and attack you with legal battles.

We had the crypto wars, and they had to be fought in court. Thankfully, we won them. There were three major legal cases to prevent the flow of information, to prevent publication of important research on cryptography, to force certain kinds of devices like the Clipper chip. Each of these fights required lots of people, lots of lawyers, lots of policymakers talking about why these issues matter.

People like Aaron Swartz, Virgil Griffith, Roman Storm, Phil Zimmerman, Chelsea Manning, Edward Snowden, Julian Assange — and many more not pictured here. Huge applause for them.

### Today's fights: chat control (10:00)

Today we have another version. Chat control in Europe is trying to take over people's private communications. This is a fight that needs to be had, a fight that people need to take on individually. You can take actions to prevent the spread of this terrible mass surveillance system.

### Building together, not fragmented (12:00)

Cypherpunks build together. Sometimes we do things on our own, but at the end of the day, we contribute our ideas and our code in open source. We refine what we do by talking about it, collaborating across many projects and teams. The Chaos Communication Congress helped convene a lot of these groups. Now the Ethereum Cypherpunk Congress convenes our group. We've also created the Cypherpunk Retreat for builders to come together and solve core shared problems.

What we want to avoid is a super-fragmented environment where nothing talks to each other and the massive centralized players win by default. If we build open standards — open identity standards, open messaging standards — we can build very large-scale privacy-preserving systems.

### Three critical open problems (14:00)

I'm going to leave you with three critical open problems for the next 10 to 15 years. Hopefully someone in this room will be inspired to work on them.

**The first is mass surveillance.** With the phones, computers, cameras, and infrastructure we've built, we now have the potential for a total mass control surveillance system way beyond anything Orwell dreamed of. When attached to social credit systems that decide what you can or can't do — whether you can access transportation, use money, fly to another country — you can establish an extremely powerful feedback system that can control billions of people. This is one of the things I fear most. We need to prevent the rise of digital totalitarian states.

**The second: the robots are coming.** The sci-fi landscape is arriving. There will be millions of robots by 2030 and billions by 2040. The companies themselves are talking about these numbers. We have five to ten years of lead time to put in place infrastructure that helps humanity navigate a world with these systems — systems that could be hacked, controlled, or used to coerce us.

**The third: building a society of humans, AIs, and uploads.** We are reaching the moment that sci-fi has talked about for a hundred years. Brain-computer interfaces, whole brain emulation — this will arrive in 15 to 30 years. We are building AGI. AGI will lead to ASI. We will be sharing the world with our digital children. What are the civilizational infrastructure components — the rules, laws, and ethics — we need to put in place to make this transition positive? This is a very hard problem. All sci-fi talks about just how damn hard it is.

I'll plug Pantheon as a phenomenal recent sci-fi that goes straight at the heart of these questions.

### Closing (17:00)

Cypherpunks — everyday normal people that do deeds to keep the darkness at bay. Please take on these open problems. We are counting on your work for the next 10, 20, 30 years to build a very positive, flourishing future.

A huge thank you to the Web3 Privacy Now community, because they've been restoring the heart of our community. I've been so sad in the crypto space to see the obsession with "token number go up." A bunch of us came into this space to establish rights and freedoms. The heart of this community is a cypherpunk one, and I am so thankful that you are here helping return these ideas. We couldn't do it without you. Thank you.
